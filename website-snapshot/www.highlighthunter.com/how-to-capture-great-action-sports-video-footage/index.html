How to Capture Great Action Sports Video Footage

04 Dec 2012 |  Posted by Noah |  0 Comment.
0

These days it doesn’t take much to create a cool video for the web – an inexpensive video camera or smartphone will do just fine.

When you’re filming, just follow these six fundamental film techniques to capture footage you’ll be proud to share.

1. Make sure there’s enough light

How many times have you thought you’ve captured a great shot only to discover the footage is too dark to use? The key is, when you’re using the sun to light your video, make sure the subject you’re filming isn’t in the shade of buildings or trees and that you shoot before the sun goes down.

2. Get the light source behind you

Make sure the light is shining either behind you as you film, or from the side of the subject. If it’s shining straight into the camera, your subject will be dark, backlit and unrecognizable.

3. Film some pre-action footage

Filming some sample clips before the action starts is a good habit to get in to make sure the lighting is adequate and that all your equipment is functioning properly.

4. Keep it steady

For steady, stable shots, consider using:

  1. Fig Rig, a circular frame with a crossbar to mount most mini DV cameras that becomes part of the body to produce smooth, steady travelling shots.
  2. Steadicam Merlin2, a lightweight, hand-held camera stabilizer system with counterbalancing weight that supports a wide range of DSLR, compact and professional camcorders, point-and-shoot cameras and other video-capture device models.
  3. If you’re on a super low-budget, use a tripod or look for a level surface like a wall or tree branch at roughly the same height as your face to rest the camera on while filming.

Any of the above options will help you capture footage that’ll be much smoother and look far more professional than you’ll get with shaky, hand-held shots.

5. Mix it up

Footage that’s all shot from the same distance gets boring quickly. Stand close to the action sometimes to get some details like facial expressions and/or equipment shots, move back a few steps to get some footage from mid-range and then go farther back to get some all-inclusive long shots. (Avoid zooming in and out if you can because this can make the viewer dizzy.) When you cut it all together, a variety of shots will be much more interesting to watch and help tell a compelling story.

6. Get longer shots than you think you’ll need

You never want to start shooting after the action has begun. Get the camera rolling before the movement begins and let it roll for a few seconds after it ends. You can always shorten the length of the footage later, especially with video editing software or a great video highlighting app.
